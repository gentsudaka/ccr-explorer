FAQ

How Prism API works, what it measures, and what it doesn't

What is Prism API?

Prism API is a serving and analysis layer built on Contextualized Construct Representations (CCR), a method for creating psychological profiles of organizations from their own public texts — earnings calls, SEC filings, annual reports. Instead of surveying individuals, Prism uses CCR to treat the organization as a "synthetic participant" and administer validated psychometric instruments to its textual output. The result is a profile across 50+ psychological dimensions (Big Five personality, Dark Triad, Schwartz Values, Moral Foundations, etc.) that captures an organization's behavioral DNA.

How are risk scores calculated?

Risk scores represent the predicted probability of DOJ criminal prosecution based on a logistic regression model combining Prism psychometric dimensions with financial controls (firm size, leverage, ROA, Tobin's Q). The model was trained on historical DOJ prosecutions and validated using leave-one-out cross-validation across 2,260 firms, achieving an AUC of 0.952. Key predictors include organizational narcissism, achievement orientation, promotion focus, and an inverse relationship with ethical risk tolerance (firms that protest ethical purity most strongly are paradoxically higher risk). Financial controls — especially firm size (log assets) — are critical: DOJ disproportionately prosecutes large firms, so size is the strongest single predictor (d=+2.35).

What data sources are used?

Organizational texts: SEC EDGAR filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy statements, earnings call transcripts). Each firm has multiple documents spanning 2018–2024.

Financial data: Compustat (S&P Capital IQ) — total assets, revenue, ROA, leverage, Tobin's Q, SIC codes. 2,263 of 2,275 firms successfully matched.

Prosecution data: DOJ criminal prosecution records, cross-referenced with SEC AAER (Accounting and Auditing Enforcement Releases) for comparison. Notably, Prism's CCR layer predicts DOJ criminal cases (AUC=0.952) but not SEC accounting fraud (AUC=0.520) — because it measures organizational culture, not individual bookkeeping fraud.

What's the difference between DOJ and AAER?

DOJ (Department of Justice) criminal prosecutions target organizational misconduct — fraud, bribery, market manipulation, environmental crimes — that reflects company-wide culture and decision-making. AAERs (SEC Accounting and Auditing Enforcement Releases) target specific accounting irregularities, often by individual actors. Prism's psychometric layer captures cultural signatures, so it predicts DOJ cases (which are cultural) but not AAERs (which are individual). This distinction is one of the key scientific findings: the method's specificity validates that it measures what it claims to measure.

Why do some "clean" companies have high risk scores?

A high risk score means the organization's psychological profile resembles firms that have been prosecuted — not that prosecution is imminent. These firms may have aggressive cultures that haven't (yet) crossed legal lines, or they may have engaged in misconduct that hasn't been detected or prosecuted. The "Top 50 Clean" table on this site deliberately highlights these cases as the most interesting: high-risk profiles without prosecution on record.

How does Prism API generate psychological scores from text?

Prism API uses CCR to simulate psychological measurement. For each dimension (e.g., Big Five Conscientiousness), a validated psychometric instrument is "administered" to the organization's text corpus. The LLM reads the organization's documents and responds to survey items as if it were the organization, producing scores on each dimension. This is analogous to how a psychologist might rate an individual after reading their extensive writings — but scaled and standardized. The method produces reliable, interpretable scores that correlate with real-world outcomes (prosecution, financial performance).

What are the limitations?

Cross-sectional: Current scores reflect a snapshot, not a time series. Organizations change.

Public texts only: Prism reads what companies choose to publish. Impression management is real — but detectable (see the "protest too much" effect in ethical risk tolerance).

U.S.-centric: Current data covers U.S. publicly traded firms with SEC filings. Extension to international markets (via local regulatory filings) is in progress.

Not causal: High risk scores predict prosecution probability, not causation. Many factors beyond organizational psychology determine whether a firm is prosecuted.

Model assumptions: The logistic regression assumes linear relationships between predictors and log-odds of prosecution. Non-linear models may capture additional patterns.
